Brand authorization · opportunity brief

Atralia has the demand. Amazon is leaking the control.

The public marketplace tells a clear story: a fragrance brand with real traction - a live Store, hero ASINs doing 1,000+ units a month, thousands of reviews, and visibility across scent-family search lanes. The commercial problem is downstream of demand: the buy path is fragmented by lower-priced offers, non-Atralia Buy Box holders, and uneven listing optimization that quietly bleeds margin and rank.

Read this honestly: public pages cannot prove whether the sellers undercutting these ASINs are unauthorized, approved distributors, old-stock arbitrage, or accounts tied to the brand. What is observable is offer fragmentation and price compression. Authorization is the lever that converts that observation into an operating plan - one owner over Buy Box, price, content, fulfillment, and ads.

Portfolio tiers & the right play per SKU

Not every SKU gets the same treatment

Treating the whole catalog with one strategy is the most common way to waste budget. Each SKU sits in a different operating state - and the state, not the ambition, dictates the lever. Scale the proven, optimize the leaky, recover the broken. Never run scale-speed on a recovery SKU, or recovery-caution on a hero.

HEROSTATE · SCALE

Scale once controlled

Absolute Noir · Sugar Mallow · Absolute Chill · Elixir.

Strongest demand and review base. Stabilize the Buy Box, polish content, then push multi-touchpoint ads. Accept a softer margin short-term to win share that compounds.

GROWTHSTATE · OPTIMIZE

Build into winners

Matcha Latte and selected collection SKUs.

Distinctive lane, early traction. Tighten content clarity, control the offers, and build review momentum before opening the ad spend.

FIXSTATE · RECOVER

Recover before scaling

Rouge Orchard · Amora Serenity · Equest Gallop.

Do not push spend into a leaky funnel. Repair trust, main image, reviews, price, and targeting first - conversion before traffic.

06

AI discovery & intent-led SEO

Built for how Amazon search works now

The next growth layer is not stuffing more keywords into titles. Amazon's search has shifted toward understanding shopper intent - who a product is for, when it's used, what problem it solves - and its AI shopping assistant now answers buyers' questions directly before they ever reach a listing. Listings that can't answer those questions lose the recommendation to ones that can.

Why this matters now: Amazon's published COSMO research describes a large-scale commonsense knowledge system deployed inside Amazon search. The practical implication: keyword matching has to be paired with intent clarity - use cases, audiences, occasions, scent families, benefits, and comparison-ready context. Amazon Science · COSMO

AI-discovery readiness - hero listings

directional, from public PDP review
Strong on identity & trust; thinnest on "how does it compare?" and "who is it for?" - the exact questions high-consideration fragrance buyers ask.

The five buyer questions every listing must answer

Amazon's assistant routes recommendations through five recurring questions. Atralia's listings answer two of them well; the other three are where recommendations leak to competitors.

1 · What is it / what does it doStrong
2 · How does it compareGap
3 · What problem does it solvePartial
4 · Who is it forGap
5 · Why should I trust itStrong

Closing the gap is cheap: backend attributes, comparison-ready bullets, audience and occasion language, and seeded Q&A - none of which touch price and most of which are invisible to the human shopper above the fold.

Intent layer

Map each ASIN to a buying job

Absolute Chill shouldn't only rank for "citrus mint perfume." It should map to jobs - summer fragrance, fresh office scent, unisex clean scent, a fresh-Pacific alternative, giftable 100ml EDP, long-lasting fresh cologne.

Semantic layer

Build context into the content

Bullets, A+ modules, image captions, and Store pages should repeat one intent architecture: scent family, audience, occasion, season, longevity, projection, bottle size, gift use.

Conversational layer

Write for natural-language queries

Shoppers ask "fresh summer cologne for men," "sweet marshmallow perfume," "long-lasting vanilla perfume gift." Listings should answer those phrases in full sentences - not only in keyword fragments.

Add to every priority listing

  • Intent bullet: who it's for and when to wear it.
  • Scent pyramid in plain language - top, heart, base.
  • Occasion modules: summer, office, date night, daily, gifting.
  • Attribute cleanup: scent, item form, volume, target audience, special feature, concentration.
  • Seeded Q&A on longevity, projection, gender fit, seasonality, similarity, bottle size.

What to avoid

  • Don't over-optimize titles until they read spammy and low-trust.
  • Don't lean on "dupe" language - it pulls traffic but erodes authority.
  • Don't scale ads into ASINs where the Buy Box is unstable.
  • Don't give every SKU the same keyword map - hero, growth, and recovery need different intent.

Listing & storefront optimization

Above the fold for humans · below it for AI
Already working
  • Brand Store is live with collection navigation.
  • Hero ASINs carry real reviews and monthly-demand signals.
  • Absolute Chill has video, imagery, a Store link, review summary, and useful scent-note coverage.
  • Titles already contain many relevant fragrance search terms.
Suppressing growth
  • Buy Box isn't consistently held by the intended operator.
  • Price gaps train shoppers to expect low-to-high $20s.
  • Uneven proof - strong heroes, thin-review tail SKUs.
  • Storefront assets aren't organized into a clear collection-level buy path.

Hero SKU optimization

Upgrade the image stack - scent pyramid, occasion, longevity, premium bottle/box cues, and comparison-safe value positioning. Keep every claim supportable.

Store merchandising

Use Store tabs as collection landing pages, not just navigation. Route ads to the right collection while shoppers are still comparing scent families.

Tail recovery

For Rouge Orchard, Amora Serenity, and thin-review SKUs: clearer title, stronger main image, policy-safe review building, and minimal ads until conversion lifts.

Advertising architecture

Feed the algorithm · harvest the winners

Modern Amazon advertising rewards giving the algorithm signal, not fighting it with narrow targeting and long negative lists. The plan runs in three layers - discover, dominate, defend - and only opens spend where the Buy Box is stable enough to keep the click.

Three-layer build - each layer runs on top of the last

discover → dominate → defend
Foundation campaigns stay live forever. Growth runs on top of Foundation; Dominance on top of both. A keyword that converts in Discover graduates into its own exact campaign in Dominate.
Sequencing rule

Earn each layer - never jump

A keyword family advances only once it has real conversion data and improving organic rank. Pushing dominance campaigns onto an unproven foundation burns spend with no signal to show for it.

Negatives, kept lean

Two reasons to negate - that's all

Obvious mismatches (wrong gender, format, use case) at build time; performance negatives only after enough clicks with zero orders. Long pre-emptive negative lists starve the algorithm of the placements it needs to find buyers.

Spend discipline

Stability gates the spend

Ads launch only where the Buy Box is reliably held - otherwise the click lands on a competing seller's offer and the budget subsidizes the leak. Control first, scale second.

The compounding move

The same proven keyword running across exact, phrase, and video placements simultaneously - each converting at a different cost - is the goal, not the redundancy. More qualified touchpoints on a winning term is how effective cost-per-click falls over time while share rises. Harvest search terms weekly; route winners into rank campaigns; prune the weeds.
